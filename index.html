METHODOLOGICAL CORE

Our work is grounded in the Plan–Do–Study–Act cycle — the methodological core of improvement science.

PDSA is a proven inquiry and improvement methodology with deep roots in healthcare, manufacturing, and education — advanced for schools through the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching, Learning to Improve, and the Networked Improvement Community model. Not a packaged intervention with a single effect size: a disciplined way to learn while doing.

PDSA · PHASE 01 OF 04

Plan — define the change worth making.

Improvement starts with a problem named in measurable terms and a working theory of how a change might move it. The Plan phase is where the consultant earns trust — before anything is tried, the question itself has to be worth answering.

  • Define the problem.Translate the felt issue into something countable — an achievement gap, a climate score, an attendance trend.
  • Analyze the data.Bring the team to what the current dashboards, surveys, and assessments already say. Let the existing evidence sharpen the problem before anyone proposes a change.
  • Develop a working theory.Name the change you'll test and the outcome you think it will move. Make the prediction explicit so it can be wrong.
  • Set the aim.What does success look like, by when, for whom — small enough to test, specific enough to recognize.
where the work begins
PDSA · PHASE 02 OF 04

Do — test the change at a scale you can learn from.

The point of Do is not implementation. It is to generate evidence about whether the working theory holds. Resist the temptation to scale before you have learned.

  • Test small.A single classroom, a single grade level, a single school site. Small is fast, fast is learning.
  • Document as it unfolds.Observations, data captures, the surprises. The mess is the data.
  • Name the conditions.What made the test feasible — staffing, time, supports. Variation is the lesson.
small enough to fail safely
PDSA · PHASE 03 OF 04

Study — read the evidence honestly, not aspirationally.

Study is the discipline of comparing prediction to result and resisting the urge to oversell either. This is the phase that protects the work from confirmation bias and from premature scaling.

  • Compare to the theory.What did the working theory predict, what actually happened, where did they diverge.
  • Surface the variation.Whose outcomes moved, whose did not, under what conditions. Averages hide the lesson.
  • Name what you do not know yet.The honest study identifies the next question as clearly as the current answer.
prediction meets result
PDSA · PHASE 04 OF 04

Act — adopt, adapt, or abandon. Then re-enter the cycle.

The cycle closes with a decision and reopens with a sharper question. Improvement is iterative by design — the next problem worth solving is rarely the one you started with.

  • Decide with discipline.Adopt the change at scale, adapt it for the next test, or abandon it cleanly. Indecision is its own decision.
  • Codify what works.Move promising practice into shared routine — not folklore, written down.
  • Re-enter the cycle.What is the next problem worth solving — narrower, sharper, informed by what you just learned.
decide. codify. begin again.

FUNDING THE WORK

The investment lives in budgets you already manage.

Most districts have multiple existing funding mechanisms that can cover this engagement — federal, state, and local. Six of the most common, with how each can pay for the work:

Pathway 01

Comprehensive Support & Improvement (CSI)

Federal Title I set-aside for schools identified through the California Dashboard. Funds can support consulting tied to a site's CSI plan — needs assessment, evidence-based interventions, monitoring.

Pathway 02

Equity Multiplier

California state funds for schools serving high-need student populations — high SED concentration, non-stability above 25%. Designed for targeted, site-specific improvement work.

Pathway 03

Differentiated Assistance (DA)

State technical-assistance funding for LEAs identified for underperforming student groups. Commonly used to bring in external coaching, data-dive protocols, and adult-learning facilitation.

Pathway 04

LCFF Supplemental & Concentration

Generated by enrollment of foster youth, English learners, and low-income students. Pays for consulting that increases or improves services for unduplicated pupils.

Pathway 05

Title II, Part A

Federal professional-learning funds. The natural home for consultant-led training, adult-learning facilitation, and instructional or leadership coaching.

Pathway 06

LREBG, ELO-P & targeted grants

One-time and recurring state grants with allowable-use clauses that include consulting for academic intervention, expanded learning, and credit recovery.

How CSI dollars pay for this work

Per the California Department of Education, LEAs receiving ESSA Section 1003 school improvement funds must spend them on evidence-based interventions and activities directly related to CSI plan development and implementation. The six allowable categories below — verbatim from CDE — map directly to our service offerings.

  • Building capacity — Adult-learning facilitation, leadership coaching.
  • Collaborating with educational partners — Advisory facilitation, stakeholder protocols.
  • Conducting needs assessments and root cause analysis — Data curation and data-dive protocols.
  • Selecting and implementing evidence-based interventions — Full-cycle PDSA consultation.
  • Using data and outcomes to monitor and evaluate improvement efforts — Custom dashboards and monitoring frameworks.
  • Reviewing, identifying, and addressing resource inequities — Budget and equity reviews tied to the CSI plan.
Source: California Department of Education — CSI LEA Authorized Use of Funds (cde.ca.gov/sp/sw/t1/csileaauthusefunds.asp).
